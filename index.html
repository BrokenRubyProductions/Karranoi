A constructed language · reference grammar

Karranoi

/kar-ra-NO-i/ — literally “tongue of waking-things”

Karranoi is a small constructed language built from the bones of the real Slappy incantation, “Karru Marri Odonna Loma Molonu Karrano,” the phrase that brings the dummy to life across the Goosebumps books, films, and the 2023 series. Every sound, letter, and grammar rule below grows out of that one phrase — this page collects all of it in one place so you can actually learn to speak it.

00 Where this comes from

Two real phrases from the Goosebumps franchise are the seed for everything here.

Karru Marri Odonna Loma Molonu Karrano wakes the dummy — “you and I are one now”
Karrano Molonu Loma Odonna Marri Karru the same words, reversed — puts him to sleep forever

That reversal is the most important fact about this language. The same words, run backward, mean the opposite. Karranoi grammar is built around that single idea — see Grammar § Reversal.

01 Sounds

Karranoi is deliberately harsh and guttural. Heavy on rolled R’s and hard consonants, with a narrow, dark vowel set — and notably, no E sound at all.

Stress rule: stress always falls on the second-to-last syllable. KAR-ru, o-DON-na, mo-LO-nu.
Doubled letters (rr, nn, etc.) mean the sound is held a beat longer or hit harder — not a different sound. This is where the dramatic rolled R lives, as in Karru.
Fun fact: the Karranoi U sound is essentially the same vowel as the Swedish letter O (as in "som" or "och") — while the Karranoi O sound matches the Lithuanian letter O (as in "oras"). Same letter, two different languages, two different sounds — which is part of why Karranoi spells its U as a dotted O in the first place.

Worked example

03 Grammar

Karranoi keeps its grammar small on purpose, so it can actually be memorized and spoken. Four pieces: word order, noun class, tense, and two short marker words for questions and possession.

Word order & the reversal rule

Ordinary sentences run Subject → Verb → Object, same as English. The distinctive rule, taken directly from the source material, is this: reversing the word order of a phrase inverts or negates its meaning. Karranoi has no separate word for “not” — instead, you flip the sentence.

Nu Vano Tova I go to the house
Tova Vano Nu I do not go to the house

Noun classes

Every noun belongs to one of three classes, marked by its final letter. The third class exists specifically for things that occupy the uncanny space between living and dead — dummies, dolls, ghosts.

ClassEndingCovers
Living–upeople, animals, anything that breathes
Inert–aobjects, tools, non-living things
Animate–dead–odummies, dolls, corpses — things that shouldn’t move, but might

Class isn’t always mechanical — a handful of words were assigned their class by feel rather than formula, the way real folklore would. Vanu (soul) is Living because a soul is the seat of life; Vanoru (ghost) is Animate–dead because it’s a soul gone wrong.

Tense

Verbs never conjugate. Tense is shown with a single marker word placed directly before the verb.

Past
ko
+ verb
Present
—
verb alone
Future
na
+ verb

Nu Lonu — I sleep · Nu Ko Lonu — I slept · Nu Na Lonu — I will sleep. The verb Lonu itself never changes shape.

Questions

Add ka to the very end of a sentence to turn a statement into a yes/no question — a spoken question mark. No word order change, no separate question words needed.

Du Vano Tova Ka — Are you going to the house?

Possession

A short word placed before the noun shows ownership.

WordMeaning
momy
doyour
lohis / her / its

Do Karano — your dummy.

Plurals

Add –i to the end of a noun to make it plural — it stacks directly onto the existing class ending, so the word still shows its noun class (living, inert, or animate-dead) at the same time as showing “more than one.”

Stelu star (singular)
Stelui stars (plural)

More examples: Horu (friend) → Horui (friends). Karano (dummy) → Karanoi (dummies) — note this lands almost identical to Karranoi, the language’s own name. A small, deliberately unsettling coincidence: many dummies and “the tongue of waking-things” sound nearly the same.

Exception — numbers override the plural: once a number comes before a noun, the noun stays singular. The number already states the quantity, so –i is dropped. Hanu Stelu — five stars (not “Hanu Stelui”). Trin Karano — three dummies.

05 Useful phrases

Ready-made lines built from the grammar and dictionary above. Speak them slowly — draw out every vowel, and let each R roll fully before moving on. Karranoi was made to be spoken unhurried, like the speaker already knows how the conversation ends.

06 English → Karranoi translator

Type an English sentence and this looks up each word in the dictionary above. It is a word-for-word lookup, not a grammar engine — Karranoi only has a couple hundred words of vocabulary, so this can’t translate everything, and it won’t apply word order, tense markers, or the reversal rule for you. Treat its output as raw material: rearrange it yourself using Grammar above (Subject–Verb–Object order, ko/na for tense, reverse the order to negate). Words it can’t find are underlined like this in the output and left in English.

Grammar note: Karranoi has no articles — there is no word for “a” or “the.” These will always show as missing, which is correct: simply drop them when speaking. “The dummy wakes” becomes just Karano Vaku.

05 Sample conversation

A short exchange using only words and grammar covered above — enough to actually practice with.

You

Halu. Namu Du Ka?

HA-lu. NA-mu du ka?

Hello. Is your name …? — literally “name you, yes/question?”

Karano

Ka. Nu Karano. Du Dravu Nu Ka?

ka. nu ka-RA-no. du DRA-vu nu ka?

Yes. I am a dummy. Do you fear me?

You

Vor. Nu Vor Dravu Du.

vor. nu vor DRA-vu du.

No. I do not fear you. — note the reversal: word order flipped to negate “Nu Dravu Du.”

Karano

Krodu. Ko Vaku Nu, Norru Kristani.

KRO-du. ko VA-ku nu, NOR-ru kris-TA-ni.

He grins. I woke, the night of the empty hour.

You

Lo Karu Lodravu Mo.

lo KA-ru lo-DRA-vu mo.

That gives me fear. — literally “it speaks my fear”

Karano

Haru. Targranu Nu Du, Norru Na Marra.

HA-ru. tar-GRA-nu nu du, NOR-ru na MAR-ra.

Good. I watch you. The night will come.
